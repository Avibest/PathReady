How to Search Effectively

1

Start with what you know about yourself

Your major, heritage, state, hobbies, intended career, religion, first-gen status, disability status â€” each can unlock specific scholarships.

2

Use reputable search platforms

Use free, well-known tools (listed below) to search. Create a profile and let the platform match you to opportunities.

3

Check locally first

Local community foundations, employers, civic organizations, and your state government often have scholarships with fewer applicants.

4

Ask your school counselor

Counselors often have a list of local and regional scholarships that never get published online.

5

Track everything in a spreadsheet

Record scholarship name, deadline, amount, requirements, and status. Missing a deadline by one day means $0.

Step-by-Step: How to Apply for Scholarships

1

Build your core materials

Prepare a resume, a general personal statement, your GPA and test scores, and 1â€“2 recommendation letter contacts before applying anywhere.

2

Read each scholarship's requirements carefully

Eligibility varies by GPA cutoff, major, residency, income, or identity. Don't waste time applying to ones you don't qualify for.

3

Tailor your essays for each application

A generic essay rarely wins. Show you understand what the scholarship values. Connect your story to their mission.

4

Apply to many â€” especially local and niche

Apply for smaller, less-known scholarships. A $500 scholarship with 20 applicants beats a $5,000 scholarship with 50,000 applicants in terms of odds.

5

Submit before the deadline and follow up

Never submit the night before. Confirm receipt when possible. Save all confirmation emails.

6

Keep applying in college

Many scholarships are renewable each year if you maintain a GPA. New scholarships also open up for current college students.

ğŸ’¡ Pro TipTreat scholarship applications like a part-time job. Set aside a few hours each week. Students who apply to 20+ scholarships statistically receive more funding than those who apply to 2â€“3.

Applications & Essays

How to Put Together a Strong Application

Your application is more than grades and test scores. It's the sum of your activities, your recommendations, and most importantly â€” your writing.

Building Your Activities List

The CommonApp gives you 10 activity slots. Use them strategically.

1

List everything first

Brainstorm every activity, job, sport, hobby, club, or responsibility you've had â€” even informal ones like caring for a sibling or managing a social media account.

2

Prioritize by impact and time commitment

Put your most significant, time-intensive, or leadership-heavy activities first. Admissions officers read top-to-bottom.

3

Use the description space wisely

150 characters per description. Be specific and quantify your impact: "Led team of 12 students" beats "Club leader."

4

Show depth, not just breadth

A few activities done for multiple years with increasing responsibility look better than 10 things done once.

ğŸ’¡ Pro TipDon't feel bad if you have fewer than 10 activities. Quality signals commitment. 6 strong entries are better than 10 weak ones.

Asking for Recommendation Letters

Writing Your Personal Statement

The personal statement (650 words, CommonApp) is your chance to speak directly to an admissions officer. It should sound like you â€” not a brochure.

1

Choose a topic that reveals something meaningful

Not necessarily the most impressive event in your life â€” the most revealing. A small story told with depth beats a grand accomplishment described vaguely.

2

Start with a scene, not a thesis

Drop readers into a specific moment. "It was 2 AM when the server crashed" is more compelling than "I have always loved technology."

3

Show, don't tell

"I am curious" tells. "I spent three weekends rebuilding a broken radio just to understand how frequency works" shows.

4

Connect the story to who you are now

How did this experience shape you? What did you learn? How does it connect to what you want to study or do in college?

5

Edit ruthlessly â€” every word earns its place

At 650 words, there's no room for filler. Cut adverbs, passive voice, and clichÃ©s. Read it out loud to catch awkward phrasing.

6

Get feedback from multiple readers

A teacher, parent, peer, and school counselor each catch different things. Don't just fix grammar â€” get input on whether the essay "sounds like you."

âš ï¸ Common Essay MistakesWriting about a sports injury or mission trip without a deeper insight. Using overly formal language that doesn't sound like you. Summarizing your resume instead of telling a story. Waiting until November to start. Using AI to write your essay â€” admissions officers can tell, and many schools will reject for this.

Career & Major Exploration

Finding Your Direction

You don't need to know exactly what you want to do at 17. But taking time to explore interests thoughtfully now will save you time, money, and stress in college.

How to Explore Your Interests

How Majors Connect to Careers

A major is a structured area of study. A career is what you do with your knowledge and skills. These don't always match up one-to-one, and that's okay.

Common MisconceptionReality
"I need to know my career before picking a major"Many careers are open to multiple majors. Skills matter as much as subject area.
"My major determines my income"Job performance, networking, and field growth also drive earnings.
"If I change my major, I wasted time"Most students change majors at least once. Core requirements often transfer.
"I need a STEM degree to be successful"Liberal arts, business, and social science majors have strong outcomes in many fields.
ğŸ’¡ Pro TipResearch the career outcomes, median salary, and job growth for any field that interests you before committing to a major. The Bureau of Labor Statistics (bls.gov) has free, reliable data on hundreds of careers.

Questions to Ask Before Choosing a Major

About Yourself

What do I enjoy learning about? What would I study even if I weren't graded? What skills do I want to develop?

About the Field

What does someone with this degree actually do day-to-day? What's the job market like? What do entry-level roles look like?

About the Major

What courses does this require? What will I be doing in class â€” research, design, analysis, writing? Do those things excite me?

About Money

What's the average starting salary? What's the growth trajectory? Can I afford the degree I need to enter this field?
